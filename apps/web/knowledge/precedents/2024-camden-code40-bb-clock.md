The motorist parked on a single yellow line in a controlled zone in Camden and displayed her Blue Badge with the photograph face down and the wheelchair symbol upwards, together with the issued cardboard clock set to the time of arrival (10:45). She returned approximately ninety minutes later to find a PCN under code 40 (waiting in a disabled persons' parking place displaying an out-of-date Blue Badge or clock).

The CEO's photographs showed the badge clearly visible on the dashboard and the clock pointer apparently set to 09:45 — one hour earlier than the motorist's stated arrival. The motorist's contention was that the rotating cardboard pointer was loose at the centre rivet and had drifted backwards under the vibration of the driver-side door being shut. The clock had been set correctly at the moment of arrival.

Camden initially refused the informal representation, citing the CEO's photograph as conclusive. The motorist made a formal representation at the NTO stage attaching: a photograph of the badge held in her hand showing it was within its validity period; a photograph of the clock with the pointer manually pushed back to the 10:45 position to demonstrate the rivet looseness; and a letter from the Blue Badge issuing authority confirming the badge had been replaced in the preceding twelve months due to a sticking pointer on the supplied clock.

Camden cancelled the PCN at the formal representation stage.

What did the work: the dual evidence — a current-validity photograph of the Badge itself plus a physical demonstration of the clock's mechanical fault — gave the council a face-saving reason to accept the appellant's account. The accompanying letter from the issuing authority pre-empted the council's most obvious rebuttal (that the appellant was inventing the drift after the fact).
