A motorist parked on a CPZ-restricted stretch in central Westminster and received a PCN under code 12 (parked in a residents' bay without a permit). The motorist held no resident permit for the zone, but contended that he had no way of identifying the bay as a resident-only bay because the small upright sign mounted on the lamp post was wholly obscured by the trailer of a large articulated lorry parked immediately upstream.

The motorist's representation included two photographs taken minutes apart from the kerb: one showed the lorry in place and the sign completely hidden, the second showed the lorry beginning to move away with the sign visible. The contemporaneous PCN photographs taken by the CEO did not include the upstream lorry, but a wider view from the council's own CCTV recorded shortly afterwards corroborated the obstruction.

The motorist also pointed out, in a single short paragraph, that the council's standard practice in adjacent CPZs is to repeat the controlling sign on the opposite side of the road or via repeater plates closer to the bay. Neither was present at this location.

Westminster cancelled the PCN at the informal representation stage with a one-line letter referring to "the obscured signage on the day" — no further reasons given. The 50% discount window was waived.

What did the work: the timestamp difference between the two photos (under ten minutes) made it implausible to allege staging, and the wider angle gave the adjudicator a single, undeniable visual fact. The legal reasoning was framed in plain English — no statutory citations were used — but the implicit argument was that a sign that cannot be seen from the bay does not adequately communicate the restriction to a stationary driver, drawing on the same line of reasoning that the Adjudicator has applied repeatedly under the Traffic Signs Regulations and General Directions.
