# TMA 2004 — the six statutory grounds

Civil parking enforcement in London is governed by the **Traffic Management Act 2004 (TMA 2004)** and the **Civil Enforcement of Parking Contraventions (England) General Regulations 2007** (as amended). Schedule 1 of the 2007 Regulations lists the **statutory grounds** on which a motorist can challenge a Penalty Charge Notice when it reaches the **formal representations** stage.

A formal representation to the council that cites one of these grounds engages a statutory process: the council must consider the ground, reject or accept it in writing, and (on rejection) issue a Notice of Rejection that gives the motorist the right to appeal to the **London Tribunals** (the adjudicator).

There are six grounds. Snappeal's letters always identify which ground (or combination) the user's facts support, and never invent grounds that don't fit the evidence.

## 1. The contravention did not occur

The motorist is saying: *the alleged act did not take place.* The bay wasn't suspended at the time. The restriction wasn't in force. The vehicle wasn't where the warden recorded it. The signs explicitly permitted what the motorist did.

This is the strongest ground when evidence supports it (photos of clear bay markings, valid suspension notices that show end-dates earlier than the PCN, etc.).

## 2. The penalty exceeds the amount applicable

The penalty as recorded on the PCN is higher than the lawful charge for the contravention and the area. Rare, but a real ground — usually only relevant where a council has misclassified the contravention band.

## 3. The recipient is not the owner

The recipient of the Notice to Owner is not the registered keeper at the relevant time. The vehicle was sold, hired out, leased to a fleet, or the keeper details on the DVLA are out of date for legitimate reasons.

## 4. The vehicle had been taken without consent

The vehicle was being driven without the registered keeper's permission (typically stolen, but also covers a family member taking the car against an explicit instruction). A police crime reference number strengthens this dramatically.

## 5. There has been a procedural impropriety

The council failed to follow the statutory procedure correctly: the PCN was served incorrectly, key required information was omitted from the PCN or Notice to Owner, time limits weren't observed, or the warden didn't follow observation requirements (e.g., minimum observation periods for "loading" contraventions).

This is a technical ground. It wins when it wins decisively, but motorists rarely have the evidence to plead it themselves.

## 6. The Traffic Order is invalid

The Traffic Regulation Order (TRO) that underpins the restriction is invalid — not properly made, not properly published, doesn't cover the location, or doesn't authorise the restriction the warden enforced. Specialist ground, occasionally decisive in residents' parking and CPZ disputes.

---

## Informal vs formal: why grounds matter at both stages

The six grounds above are the **statutory** grounds — they are the grounds the **adjudicator** must consider at the final stage. But the **informal representations** stage (the first stage, while the discount window is still open) is **not** restricted to the statutory grounds. At informal-rep stage a council reviewer can cancel a PCN on **mitigating circumstances** alone:

- Medical emergency
- Active loading or unloading
- Mechanical breakdown
- Blue Badge displayed correctly
- Valid permit obscured but present
- Signage hidden, faded, or contradictory

These informal grounds are not in TMA 2004, but they are how a large fraction of London PCNs are actually cancelled — at the discretion of council reviewers. **The right strategy is usually: lead with the strongest informal ground at the informal stage, then escalate to the relevant statutory ground if rejected.**
