# What good evidence looks like

Last refreshed **2026-05-27 (v0.3.10)**.

The short version:

- **Take more photos than you think you need.** Six is the cap; six is also about right.
- **Wide first, then close.** A wide shot of the street establishes context; close shots of signs, markings, and the car itself prove specific claims.
- **Photograph what's missing.** A missing sign or a hidden one is worth more than a present one. If a sign is behind a tree, photograph the tree.
- **Timestamps matter.** Don't disable timestamps in your camera. The metadata is the evidence.
- **Don't stage anything.** A council reviewer can tell when a photo is set up. Don't move cones, don't reposition the car, don't re-park.

## How evidence affects the AI strength score

The drafter returns a 0–100 strength score with every letter. The calibration is deliberately tied to the evidence base:

| Evidence supplied | Typical score band |
|---|---|
| Right ground + relevant photo + ≥ 50 chars of notes describing what happened | **80–100 — Strong** (green badge) |
| Right ground + EITHER a photo OR ≥ 50 chars of notes | **50–79 — Solid** (amber badge) |
| No evidence photos AND notes < 50 chars | **capped at 45 — Weak** (red warning above Pay button) |

The cap exists so the AI can't oversell a thin case. To lift a sub-50 score:

1. **Add at least one photo.** A clear shot of the sign / marking / suspension notice that supports your chosen ground is the highest-impact thing you can add.
2. **Write at least one full sentence in notes.** "I returned to the car at 14:05 and the suspension sign was missing — the only sign at the bay was the standard residents' permit sign" beats "sign missing".
3. **Use voice dictation if typing is hard.** The dictation panel transcribes 30 s of audio in a couple of seconds and you can keep adding takes.

The red weak-appeal warning isn't a hard gate — you can still pay £2.99 and submit. But the warning's specific evidence asks (e.g. "Add a photo of the suspension sign", "Describe what was blocking the sign") usually lift the score into the amber band in a few minutes if the evidence actually exists.
